Philosophy

Why I build this way

Not a mission statement. Just the thinking that's held up, project after project.

Every project on this site started as a question, not a plan. These are the habits that turned that curiosity into something worth documenting.

01

Why I document everything

A working project with no notes is a project only I can maintain — and only for as long as I remember why I made each decision. Writing it down, including the boring parts, is what makes it useful to future-me and to anyone else.

02

Why I use AI, but verify its output

AI is a fast way to get a first draft of an idea, a config, or a paragraph — not a source of truth. Every suggestion gets checked against real behavior before it's kept. The judgment stays mine; the acceleration is what AI is for.

03

Why mistakes are documented instead of hidden

The polished version of a project teaches you almost nothing. What went wrong, and why, is usually the most useful part of the record — for me on the next project, and for anyone reading who's stuck on the same problem.

04

Why old hardware deserves a second life

A decade-old office PC with a modern GPU dropped in is still a capable machine. Most of what I've learned about systems, thermals, and constraints came from working within hardware limits rather than buying around them.

05

Why building teaches more than consuming

Tutorials show you a path that already worked for someone else. Building something from a real question means hitting the parts tutorials skip — which is exactly where the actual learning happens.

The constitution

Every project should

  • Begin with a real question.
  • Solve a real problem.
  • Document both success and failure.
  • Clearly distinguish AI-generated ideas from human verification.
  • Be understandable by someone discovering the project for the first time.
  • Leave a better record than existed before the project began.
